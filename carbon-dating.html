Carbon Dating

Introduction

Radiocarbon dating, also known as carbon-14 dating or simply carbon dating, is used to calculate the approximate ages of carbon-based artefacts such as plants, bones or clothes.

Quick Refresher: What are Cosmic Rays?

  • Most often from outside our Solar System, but their origins, other than from supernovae, are yet to be found.
  • They are sub-atomic fragments containing protons, few electrons and atomic nuclei including helium nucleus (alpha particles) or elements as heavy as lead.
    • Roughly 90% are hydrogen protons and 9% are alpha particles, the two most abundant elements in the universe.

When cosmic rays collide with atoms and molecules (mainly oxygen and nitrogen) in the Earth’s atmosphere, they form neutrons (zero protons, one proton). When these neutrons collide with nitrogen-14 (seven protons, seven neutrons), it replaces one of the nitrogen’s protons with itself, increasing the nitrogen-14 to 8 neutrons and reducing it to 6 protons. The replaced proton is emitted as a hydrogen atom (one proton, zero neutrons). By definition (number of protons defines an element), since it now only has 6 protons, it is now a radioactive, unstable isotope, carbon-14, due to its massive nucleus. Although carbon-12 is the stable, most abundant element of carbon on Earth, carbon-14 also enters the carbon cycle through various means such as photosynthesis (after combining with oxygen to form carbon dioxide, which plants intake) and then through the food chain, reaching humans as well. Once the organism dies, they no longer absorb carbon-14 as they can no longer consuming food. The remaining carbon-14 will decay by beta minus decay to form nitrogen-14, an electron and an electron antineutrino. The rate of this decay is given by its half-life, equivalent to around 5,370 years for carbon-14.
