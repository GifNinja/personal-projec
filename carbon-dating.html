Carbon Dating

Background Knowledge: Basic understanding of radioactive decay (chemistry), basic exponential and logarithmic manipulation
Keywords: Radioactive decay, half-life, carbon-14.

Applications

Radiocarbon dating, also known as carbon-14 dating or simply carbon dating, is used to calculate the approximate ages of carbon-based artefacts such as plants, bones or clothes.

Quick Refresher: What are Cosmic Rays?

  • It is unknown where exactly they are from, but most cosmic rays are from supernovae outside our Solar System.
  • They are sub-atomic fragments containing protons, few electrons and atomic nuclei including helium nucleus (alpha particles) or elements as heavy as lead.
  • Roughly 90% are hydrogen protons and 9% are alpha particles, the two most abundant elements in the universe.

So... what's the deal with carbon-14?

How is carbon-14 formed?

  1. When cosmic rays collide with atoms and molecules (mainly oxygen and nitrogen) in the Earth’s atmosphere, they form neutrons
  2. When this neutron in the atmosphere collides with nitrogen-14 (also in the atmosphere), it replaces one of the nitrogen-14’s protons with itself. As a result, the nitrogen-14 is increased to to 8 neutrons and reduced to 6 protons.
  3. The replaced proton is emitted as a hydrogen atom (one proton, zero neutrons).
  4. By definition, since the number of protons defines an element on the period table, it is now a radioactive isotope of carbon-14 .

How does it enter the Earth then?

Although carbon-12 is the stable, most abundant element of carbon on Earth, carbon-14 also enters the carbon cycle through various means such as photosynthesis (after combining with oxygen to form carbon dioxide, which plants intake) and then through the food chain, reaching humans as well. Once the organism dies, they no longer absorb carbon-14 as they are no longer consuming food.

What happens to the carbon-14 now?

The remaining carbon-14 will decay by beta minus decay to form nitrogen-14, an electron and an electron antineutrino. The rate of this decay is given by its half-life, equivalent to around 5,370 years for carbon-14.

Mathematics

Given the exponential decay formula $A=A_0e^{-kt}$ where $A$ = number of atoms left after time $t$, $A_0$ = number of atoms of the isotope in the original sample, $k$ = rate constant for radioactive decay,

$\frac{A}{A_0} =e^{-kt}$
$ln(\frac{A}{A_0}) =ln⁡(e^{-kt}) =-kt$
$t=\frac{ln(\frac{A}{A_0}))}{-k}$

To find $k$, note that when $t$ is at its half-life, $A$ will be half of $A_0$ (definition of half-life).

$t_{1/2}=\frac{ln(1/2)}{-k}$
$t_{1/2}=\frac{-ln(2)}{t_{1/2}}$
$k=\frac{ln(2)}{t_{1/2}}$

Substituting $k$,

$t=\frac{ln(\frac{A}{A_0})}{-\frac{ln(2)}{t_{1/2}}}$
$t=\frac{ln(\frac{A}{A_0})}{-ln(2)}*t_{1/2}$

Note: $k$ can also be found with calculus as it is a first-order differential reaction, but it is beyond the scope of this article which will focus on the applications of exponentials and logarithms.
